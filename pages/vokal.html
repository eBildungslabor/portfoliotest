Vokal

After graduating from college, I decided to spend the summer traveling around Europe for a month on my own. I had never done anything like this before. This would be my first time travelling solo for an extended period of time, and I had decided to take the plunge and do it internationally. I had planned for every eventuality—choosing hostels, arranging transportation, bookmarking attractions, and making far, far too many lists in general—or so I thought.

I stepped off the plane in Madrid and my phone didn’t work. This wouldn’t have been a big deal if not for the fact that a) the only thing I can do in Spanish is count to ten, b) all of my directions and carefully-laid plans were on that phone, and c) I had no way of communicating with anyone. Of all of these issues, it was the lack of communication which induced the most panic. I had known that I would be traveling alone, but now I was reeeeeeally on my own with no way to make a local phone call, let alone get in touch with anyone back home.

Eventually, the matter was solved (thank goodness), but I still remember that overwhelming feeling of dread. When I had settled back in on my side of the ocean and started getting involved in UX, this was one of the first problems I sought to address. How can I make sure this never happens to anyone else? I wondered.

Vokal is my solution.

Proposed Solution

Vokal is a bright and engaging mobile messenger application. It’s my answer to common instant messaging usability problems, including texting and data limitations, international communication fees, lack of interface customization, and unreliable phone service.

Scroll on to learn more about how I arrived at this solution.

Process & Challenges

User Research

Users

Since my international service issue was incredibly specific and not widely applicable to other users, I first sought to expand the user base. As a college student, I had been constantly surrounded by peers trying out—and complaining about—the latest messenger apps, so I had plenty of feedback to build my solution upon. I decided that Vokal would best appeal to users between the ages of 12 and 30—a user base of students and young professionals looking to communicate with friends and organize group activities.

Problems & Fears

As a recent convert to the modern phone (goodbye, my old brick phone), I was well aware of many functional limitations of older phone models. I wanted to take these limitations into consideration and offer a solution which surmounted them. I also wanted to tackle a lot of common complaints I had heard from my peers about their messenger apps, and provide a fix for my international issue.

Thus, my final list of user-defined problems and fears (and potential solutions to them) looked something like this:

  • user has limited texting / phone capability → app uses wifi / data and can function without phone service
  • user dislikes traditional user interface and features of default messenger → app provides ample opportunity for customization of interface
  • user has an outdated phone which does not support group text or "fun/cool" apps, so the user is sometimes left out of the loop → app is supported on outdated phone models and offers group chat functionality
  • user finds international communication expensive and unreliable → app is not dependent on service provider, and is capable of functioning internationally
  • user lost phone and all contacts → app saves contacts to your account – contacts can be retrieved and restored on any device
  • user is in a hurry or lazy and worries that app will be difficult to use → app has a simple, intuitive functionality that won’t waste time
  • user is afraid the app won’t work or will be slow and buggy → app will undergo vigorous user testing and will receive regular support updates and maintenance based on user feedback
  • user is concerned about the privacy of their information and conversations → all information on the app is confidential, and screen notifications can be customized or disabled
  • user is afraid the app will use too much data → app uses the minimum amount of data necessary to function

Motives

What drives a user to actually use something? Here are my thoughts on why a user would want to use Vokal.

  • user wants to stay in touch with friends
  • user wants to be included in group activities
  • user wants to customize look of messenger
  • user wants to follow current trends and use "fun/cool" new ap

Alternatives

There are plenty of alternative messenger apps already, so why reinvent the wheel? Well, maybe the wheel is deflated or actually triangular and not much of a wheel at all. Lots of the messenger apps my peers were using made big promises but didn’t actually deliver on them. I examined some of the popular alternative options already in existence to determine their shortcomings.

  • default messenger → almost never has customization options, can be clunky and unreliable, might not support group or picture messages, might not function internationally
  • alternate app: Skype → lacks customization options, heavy on data usage, simple processes (such as adding contacts or sending media messages) can be unnecessarily complex or unreliable, not well supported on outdated phones, has lots of bugs and limited mobile functionality
  • alternate app: WhatsApp → very minimal customization options, not well supported or available on outdated phones, notifications not always reliable
  • simply make a phone call → most users find this a time-consuming and inefficient method of communication, especially for organizing group activities; international phone calls are very expensive

In response to these shortcomings, it was obvious that Vokal would need to be supported and available on older phones, use little data, offer calling and group messaging features, have international functionality, and offer a simple, intuitive, and customizable user interface.

Information Architecture

Most of my ideas are born as messy pencil scribbles on a post-it note. I roughly sketch the general layout of the design and make notes on different features or functions I’d like to include. Once I’ve got a clearer idea of what needs to happen, I go back and create a wireframe, placing things more definitively. I tend to prefer doing this with pen and paper too—it just feels more intuitive, and you can’t beat having an eraser.

These sketches and wireframes inevitably get tweaked as I transition to creating a high-fidelity prototype in Photoshop. I find it a lot easier (and more fun!) to envision a final product once there’s a bit of color on the drawing board.

Why "Vokal"? Well, I’m an English major, and one of the unspoken rules about having a degree in English is that you need to have an appreciation for puns. It’s that simple. "Get Vokal" was simply too good to pass up as a tagline.
